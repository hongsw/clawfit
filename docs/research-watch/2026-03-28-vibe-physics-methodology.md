# Research Watch: vibe-physics as an AI-native research methodology signal

- Source: <https://www.anthropic.com/research/vibe-physics>
- Title: **Vibe physics: The AI grad student**

## Why this is worth watching
This is important because it points to something larger than a product launch.
It signals that **development/research methodology itself is becoming a first-class layer** in the AI ecosystem.

The key shift is this:

> the question is no longer only what tool or model to use,
> but what methodology lets a human and AI work together effectively on complex domain problems.

## What stands out in the article
The article is striking because it frames AI not as a full autonomous scientist, but as something closer to a supervised graduate student.

Important elements include:
- a human expert supervising the process
- a real research problem chosen with bounded scope
- strict operational rules
- text-prompt-only interaction
- decomposition into many tasks and markdown artifacts
- domain expertise remaining necessary for evaluation

This is highly relevant because it does **not** describe magic full autonomy.
It describes a structured methodology for getting frontier-useful work out of an AI system.

## Most important methodological lessons
### 1. Prompting is not the method
The success described here is not about one clever prompt.
It is about a structured, staged process.

### 2. Supervision remains central
The article strongly suggests domain expertise still matters for:
- problem selection
- evaluation
- correction
- deciding what counts as a valid result

### 3. File structure and decomposition matter
The use of a task tree, markdown artifacts, and staged plans looks a lot like an emerging methodology pattern.
It resembles the same broader movement seen in:
- harness engineering
- executable SSOT
- agent task decomposition
- structured subagent workflows

### 4. AI-native research methods are becoming visible
This may be one of the clearest public signs that domain-specific AI work is developing its own recognizable methodology language.
