# clawfit adoption levels

This document tracks a different axis from `reference-levels.md`.

- `reference-levels.md` asks: **what layers exist in the ecosystem?**
- `adoption-levels.md` asks: **how far has a person, team, or organization actually integrated AI into real work?**

This matters because two teams can use the same tools and still be at very different stages of AI maturity.

---

## Level 0 — No AI
AI is not meaningfully part of the workflow.

Typical signs:
- no regular AI usage in day-to-day work
- experiments are rare or absent
- no shared AI practices
- existing software / operational workflows run without AI involvement

This is the true baseline.

---

## Level 1 — Casual prompting
AI is used occasionally, usually in a loose chat-style way.

Typical signs:
- ad hoc prompting
- one-off question answering
- little persistence or repeatability
- mostly individual usage

AI is present, but not yet integrated.

---

## Level 2 — Repeatable individual usage
An individual starts developing repeatable habits and patterns.

Typical signs:
- recurring prompt strategies
- regular use for coding / writing / planning / debugging
- early personal workflows
- some awareness of which tasks AI is good or bad at

AI becomes a real personal tool.

---

## Level 3 — Structured workflow usage
AI use becomes more structured and task-aware.

Typical signs:
- explicit task decomposition
- repeated use of tools / context injection / model selection
- deliberate workflow shaping
- outputs reviewed against clearer expectations

AI is no longer just used; it is being operationalized.

---

## Level 4 — Integrated tool + workflow layer
AI is now embedded in repeatable workflows and local systems.

Typical signs:
- use of coding agents, tool-use systems, memory/context layers
- project-specific prompts or instructions
- workflow integration into daily execution
- stronger distinction between simple prompting and structured usage

At this stage, AI is part of the working stack.

---

## Level 5 — Harness-aware usage
The user/team starts thinking in terms of harnesses, wrappers, and reusable operating layers.

Typical signs:
- adoption of wrappers / meta-layers / opinionated workflows
- reusable commands, skills, or project instructions
- stronger concern for consistency, not just output quality
- moving beyond raw tool usage toward an operating model

This is where the harness layer becomes visible.

---

## Level 6 — Orchestrated multi-agent work
AI is coordinated as a system of agents rather than a single assistant.

Typical signs:
- subagents / agent teams / parallel execution
- role decomposition
- dependency tracking
- verification and quality gates
- asynchronous coordination

At this stage, the human is often closer to an orchestrator than a direct operator.

---

## Level 7 — Team system / executable SSOT / organizational layer
AI becomes part of how the team itself works.

Typical signs:
- team-wide workflow packs
- shared rules / skills / commands
- executable documentation / executable SSOT
- governance, review, approval structures
- raising the floor across the team, not just raising individual output

This is where AI usage becomes organizational infrastructure.

---

## Level 8 — Methodology-native human-AI collaboration
AI is no longer just a tool or workflow layer; it becomes part of a repeatable methodology.

Typical signs:
- explicit methods for human-AI collaboration
- structured research/build methodologies
- feedback loops designed into the process
- methodology language spreading across the team/community
- AI-native ways of working (e.g. harness engineering, methodology signals, supervised multi-stage collaboration)

At this level, the important question is not just what tool is used, but what **method** governs the collaboration.

---

## Why Level 0 matters
Adding **Level 0 — No AI** is important because it provides a true baseline.
Without Level 0, every maturity model starts too late and makes early adoption look more advanced than it really is.

Level 0 keeps the ladder honest.

---

## Relationship to reference layers
A team can be:
- high on adoption, but narrow in ecosystem awareness
- low on adoption, but deeply aware of the ecosystem
- strong in orchestration, but weak in methodology
- strong in base runtime usage, but weak in team harnesses

This is why `clawfit` needs both:
1. **reference layers** (what exists)
2. **adoption levels** (how far usage has evolved)

These are different maps.
